There's always something you can do to make a difference, no matter how big or little your garden is or how much expertise you have. Here are our nine suggestions for making your landscape more eco-friendly.


1. Filling your garden with as many native plants as possible is one of the best strategies to attract animals. Native wildflowers are easier to grow and manage than non-natives, and they are often more pest resistant. They're also great for attracting bees and butterflies, which are in desperate need of support due to population declines across the country.


2. Plant a tree If you have space, why not plant a tree? Research has shown that gardens with trees are more attractive to wildlife than those without. They can provide additional benefits like shade and reduced noise pollution too. Again, native species will deliver the greatest benefits.


3. Get a little wild It's fair that you want to maintain your garden looking nice, but an overly-kept environment leaves little room for wildlife. Allow an area of your garden to become overgrown, and you'll have created a more appealing environment for all things wild by allowing nature to take its course.


4. Make wildlife shelters By offering some man-made shelter, you can provide a hand to nature. There are many methods to make your yard more wildlife-friendly, from bird boxes to insect motels, hedgehog nests to bee hives. Take a look at our online store or try your hand at making your own.


5. Reduce trash It's easy to generate unneeded waste while gardening, but happily, finding more ecologically friendly options is just as straightforward. For example, instead of drinking from the tap, you can save water by collecting rainwater.


6. Instead of collecting fallen leaves, put them in a quiet area to provide a warm winter haven for small animals. Once they've decayed, you'll have natural leaf mold, which is an excellent fertilizer.


7. You can produce your own fertilizer in a variety of ways, including using natural leaf mould. To develop a natural manner of aiding your garden's growth, you can use anything from leftover food to pulled-up weeds and grass cuttings. Weeds soaked for a few weeks in a container provide a nutrient-rich solution that can be used to irrigate plants. You might also try 'grass cycling,' which is leaving grass clippings on the lawn after mowing to act as a natural fertilizer.


8. Compost heaps are a surprising useful habitat element for wildlife, and they can assist to attract a wide range of critters to your yard, from earthworms and frogs to hedgehogs and lizards. Never use peat in your compost since harvesting it might harm unique ecosystems that take decades to develop.


9. Add a water feature to your landscape If you have the space, a natural water feature can make a big difference. A pond provides a home for frogs and insects, as well as water for birds and mammals to drink and bathe. If you don't have enough space for a full-fledged pond, a bird bath or even a shallow dish of water placed on the ground can suffice. Improve access Modern gardens are rapidly enclosing themselves with enormous walls and fences, making it difficult for ground-dwelling animals like hedgehogs to navigate. To get in and out of your garden, a hog only needs a 13cm by 13cm opening, so consider creating a small hole in your fence to help this vulnerable species.
